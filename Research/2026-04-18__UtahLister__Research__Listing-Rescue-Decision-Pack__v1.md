# Listing Rescue Decision Pack

Research date: April 18, 2026

This is the clean public-safe synthesis of the April 18 rescue research set.

It consolidates five separate research threads:

- stale-listing failure analysis
- outreach and platform-risk boundaries
- rescue intake design
- rescue pricing
- online image usage policy

## Bottom line

`Listing Rescue` is worth adding as an outbound wedge, but it should not replace the core UtahLister business.

Best operating rule:

- keep UtahLister as a dual-offer service business
- use `Listing Rescue` as a controlled acquisition wedge
- keep rescue manual and permission-based at first
- do not scale rescue outreach through automation

## Locked business decisions

### 1. Business model

UtahLister remains one business with two entry paths:

- `New Listing Build`
- `Listing Rescue`

`Listing Rescue` is not a pivot. It is a narrower entry path for sellers with live listings that are stale, underpriced, weakly framed, or missing critical detail.

### 2. Outreach policy

Manual rescue outreach is allowed only as a small pilot.

Rules:

- one seller, one touch
- no follow-up unless they reply
- no fake buyer angle
- no links in message one
- no automation, scraping, or bulk outreach

Practical conclusion:

- Facebook Marketplace DMs are high-risk
- KSL seller DMs are medium-risk
- official channels such as KSL Service Listings and paid Meta surfaces are better scale paths

### 3. Rescue intake model

Use a hybrid two-step intake:

- `Step 1`: quick rescue screen
- `Step 2`: dynamic follow-up based on category and failure pattern

The rescue path should be diagnosis-first, not creation-first.

### 4. Rescue pricing

Current recommended test structure:

- `Rescue Audit`: `$19`
- `Standard One-Item Rescue`: `$29`
- `Bulky / Complex Rescue`: `$49`
- `Premium / Custom Rescue`: `$79+`
- `Simple multi-item packs`: `3 for $79`, `5 for $129`

Pricing should stay flat-fee and productized. Avoid commissions and success-fee framing.

### 5. Image policy

Actual-item photos must lead.

Official rule:

- the first image should be an actual-item photo
- stock or manufacturer images should not be used as the hero image
- a later-gallery reference image is only acceptable for new, sealed, standardized items with disclosure and manual approval

## What weak listings most often get wrong

The highest-frequency fixable problems were:

1. generic titles
2. thin descriptions
3. missing dimensions and moveability details
4. distressed "need gone" pricing language
5. weak or unclear cover photos
6. bundle confusion
7. missing brand or retail-anchor detail

This makes rescue strongest in categories where clarity and logistics matter:

- furniture
- bulky household items
- dressers, hutches, tables, benches, shelving, and cabinets
- select outdoor gear and branded consumer items

## Best rescue targets

Strong early rescue targets:

- listings live for 14 or more days
- one or more price drops
- generic titles
- missing size, condition, or transport information
- better-looking items than the listing suggests

Poor rescue targets:

- junk items
- extremely low-value items
- clearly damaged inventory with little upside
- sellers who want guaranteed sale outcomes

## Operating implications for UtahLister

These decisions should shape the business and product stack:

- website should support both service paths
- rescue intake should ask for live listing URL or screenshots first
- internal tooling should support rescue diagnosis separately from new-listing generation
- public proof should emphasize clarity, pricing discipline, and seller control

## Public-safe summary

The research supports a simple conclusion:

`UtahLister should keep the core service the same, add Listing Rescue as a tightly controlled wedge, and validate it through proof before trying to scale it.`
