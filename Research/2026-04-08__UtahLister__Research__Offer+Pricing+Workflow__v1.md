**RECOMMENDATION (best practical “no-password, maximum done-for-you” workflow):**

**Step 1 — Customer purchases and chooses platform(s).**  
Customer selects one of 2–3 clear packages (not 10). This reduces decision paralysis and sets expectations correctly. citeturn5search17turn5search13

**9. Recommended Turnaround and Revision Rules**

**FACTS (why revisions must be bounded):**
Professional services contracts commonly treat revisions/additions outside the agreed scope as additional billable services, and they treat client delays (late materials/approvals) as schedule-impacting. citeturn19view0  
OfferUp’s ecosystem shows that listing accuracy and clarity reduce problems and back-and-forth; and response behavior affects reputation. That means revisions can become a bottomless pit if not constrained. citeturn14view0turn21view0

**RECOMMENDATION (turnaround promises that are realistic for a solo operator):**
- Standard: **48 hours** after complete intake (photos + required fields).  
- Rush add-on: **24 hours** (or “next business day”) with a surcharge. (Rush surcharge concept is standard in service contracts.) citeturn19view0  
- “Incomplete intake” pauses the clock automatically. citeturn19view0turn10view0

**RECOMMENDATION (revision policy that avoids scope creep):**
- Include **1 revision round** (defined narrowly: edits to wording, re-ordering details, adjusting price within the same pricing strategy).  
- Exclude from included revisions:
  - new photos after delivery,
  - platform change (e.g., “actually I want eBay too”),
  - major repositioning (“now make it luxury boutique vibe”),
  - adding missing specs the customer didn’t provide initially,
  - splitting/combining bundles.
These become paid “change orders” (add-on fees). This mirrors standard change-order logic in professional services agreements. citeturn19view0turn5search28

**RECOMMENDATION (post-delivery support rule):**
If UtahLister offers post-delivery message coaching, cap it by **time window** (e.g., 7 days) and by **volume** (e.g., up to 10 message replies coached). Otherwise, it becomes an unlimited retainer accidentally. OfferUp’s guidance that quick messages matter will create demand—so you must price and bound it. citeturn14view0turn21view0

## Pricing Strategy and Profit Floor

**10. Pricing Model Analysis**

**FACTS (pricing transparency matters for trust and conversion):**
In usability research, customers become frustrated and leave when pricing is absent or unclear; showing prices (or clear sample prices) can be a competitive advantage because pricing is a primary information need. citeturn5search33turn5search29

**INFERENCE:** UtahLister needs fixed, transparent pricing to be trust-building and low-friction—especially because the service is operationally simple (per-item listing packs) and early-stage customers will not tolerate consultative “it depends” quoting for something they perceive as commoditized.

**Pricing model options (with solo-operator tradeoffs):**

**Per item (flat price).**  
**Pros:** simplest; easiest to buy; easiest to fulfill.  
**Cons:** can undercharge complex items unless there’s gating.

**Per complexity tier (simple vs complex).**  
**Pros:** protects margin; aligns to time drivers (research intensity, specs, fraud risk).  
**Cons:** requires a clear definition of “complex” to prevent disputes.

**Per value tier (based on target sale price).**  
**Pros:** connects to customer value; higher willingness-to-pay for high-ticket items.  
**Cons:** invites arguments and requires proof/estimates; can feel like “commission” without actual selling.

**Bundles (3-pack / 5-pack).**  
**Pros:** increases AOV and reduces per-order admin; predictable workload blocks.  
**Cons:** needs bundle constraints (must be submitted together).

**Subscription / monthly support.**  
**Pros:** stable revenue; ideal for resellers.  
**Cons:** dangerous for solo operator unless strictly bounded (otherwise it becomes unlimited labor).

**Commission-based (percent of sale).**  
**FACTS:** Commission models exist in consignment; for example, entity["company","eBay","ecommerce marketplace, us"] consignment program terms describe partner commissions in the 20–40% range (and may include additional fees). citeturn6search23  
**INFERENCE:** Commission is mismatched for UtahLister’s “listing-only, no-handling” model because the founder can’t control sale outcomes, buyer behavior, meetups, or fulfillment. Commission also complicates “no password” and tracking.

**RECOMMENDATION:** Start with **per item + complexity tiers + bundles**, and keep any subscription as a later-stage offering only after time-per-client is proven. citeturn5search7turn5search17turn5search33

**INFERENCE (time is the main cost driver):**
For a solo founder, labor time + context switching is the real unit cost. **RECOMMENDATION (use a price-floor formula, not vibes):**

Let:
- **T** = all-in time per item (hours), including messaging/revisions/admin  
- **R** = required hourly revenue (not wage—revenue that covers overhead + taxes + profit)  
- **F** = payment fee rate (e.g., 0.029)  
- **C** = fixed per-transaction fee (e.g., $0.30)  
- **B** = buffer for refunds/disputes/support (e.g., 10–20% of price)

Then a practical floor is:

**Price ≥ (T × R + C) / (1 − F − B)** citeturn2search0turn2search2

**Example (not a fact—illustrative math):**
If T=0.75 hours (45 min), R=$60/hr, F=2.9%, C=$0.30, B=15% buffer, then:  
Price ≥ ($45 + $0.30) / (1 − 0.029 − 0.15) ≈ $55.  
This implies a $49 “simple item” price only works if the true all-in time is closer to ~35–40 minutes or the required hourly revenue is lower.
