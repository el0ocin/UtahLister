## 2. Executive Summary  
UtahLister must adopt explicit, data-driven quality rules for each listing component.  For example, **Title** guidelines should mimic top marketplaces: keep titles ≤150 chars, include brand/model/size (properly capitalized), and exclude irrelevant or promotional text【6†L137-L142】【31†L269-L278】.  **Descriptions** should clearly spell out item features and condition (typically ≥150 words), focusing on benefits and using bullet points or short paragraphs for readability【6†L165-L173】【31†L280-L287】.  **Photo guidance** requires multiple high-resolution images on neutral backgrounds【7†L58-L63】【9†L91-L97】 (research shows ~7 photos yield peak conversions【3†L354-L360】).  **Pricing recommendations** must be justified with comparable sales (e.g. eBay “sold” data【22†L263-L272】) and adjusted for condition/costs【30†L428-L436】.  **Platform and category** suggestions should be justified by item type and marketplace rules (wrong category drastically cuts visibility【14†L54-L63】【9†L57-L64】).  

We propose a **QC system** with checklists and pass/fail logic: every draft is inspected against these criteria (e.g. no banned terms, all fields filled) before delivery.  Any violation (missing mandatory info or policy breach) causes immediate rework.  Internal controls will include templates/automations for consistency and periodic audits of past listings【3†L319-L327】【41†L38-L44】.  We also distinguish true defects (breach of standards) from mere preference revisions【35†L657-L660】, so that only objective QC fixes are done under warranty.  Overall, the framework blends clear spec-driven rules (drawn from [4,6,9,22]) with the flexibility needed for creative deliverables.  Below we detail standards, common pitfalls, pass/fail rules, controls, and metrics.

## 3. Recommended Quality Philosophy for UtahLister  
UtahLister’s quality philosophy should be **spec-driven and customer-focused**.  In other words: **define “good” explicitly and measure it**【41†L29-L36】.  Quality is not vague “be detail-oriented” fluff but clear, objective requirements (e.g. “title must list brand and key attributes”) drawn from buyer needs and platform rules.  For example, Walmart explicitly ties good listings to correct categories, detailed content and images【4†L203-L209】.  We adopt a similar mindset: each listing must satisfy a checklist of things a buyer expects (brand, specs, clear photo) and marketplace demands (allowed content).  

Continuous improvement is key: use **feedback loops and audits**.  UtahLister should sample past listings regularly (weekly or monthly audits【3†L319-L327】【41†L38-L44】) to catch drift and refine standards.  This echoes the advice to avoid “incorrect definition of quality” by collaborating with customers and reviewing processes【41†L29-L36】【41†L38-L44】.  We also plan to treat the solo operator as both creator and inspector: invest in training (even self-training) and use tools (spell-checkers, image scanners).  In sum, we promote a humble but exacting culture: *tell it like it is* to the client (no sugar-coating flaws) and *be consistent* in how “good” is judged.  The emphasis is on **functionality for the customer** (a listing that converts) rather than subjective flair.

## 4. Core Quality Standards Across the Service  
Across all deliverables, UtahLister should enforce these universal standards:
- **Clarity and Relevance**: Text should read naturally and include the right keywords. Titles and descriptions must answer a buyer’s questions (brand, type, condition) without filler. Avoid jargon or keyword-stuffing【31†L280-L287】【3†L197-L204】.  
- **Accuracy & Completeness**: All product attributes must be filled (e.g. size, color, material). Platforms favor complete data【4†L203-L209】【28†L69-L77】. Omitting details (like a smartphone’s storage or item’s brand) confuses buyers and hurts ranking【28†L69-L77】.  
- **Consistency & Professionalism**: Use consistent styling (title casing or sentence case per platform), correct grammar/spelling【9†L73-L81】, and brand voice. For images, ensure uniform background and lighting【7†L58-L63】.  E.g., one guide notes buyers expect white/clean backgrounds and image continuity【7†L125-L134】.  
- **Compliance**: Follow each marketplace’s rules. For instance, avoid prohibited promotions or all-caps in titles【6†L137-L142】【31†L275-L278】. Walmart specifically prohibits terms like “100%” or “guaranteed” in listings【6†L144-L152】. Noncompliance can lead to unpublishing【6†L118-L126】.  
- **Visual Quality**: High-resolution images are non-negotiable. Ensure the product fills about 85% of the frame【7†L125-L134】, with the main image centered and additional shots showing every angle. Research shows each extra clear image significantly boosts conversion【3†L354-L360】【9†L91-L97】.  
- **Reasoned Pricing**: Price must match market realities. Use real data: compare completed sales, factor in condition and fees【22†L263-L272】【30†L428-L436】. A random or obviously high/low price is a red flag.

These core principles are mandatory baseline rules—every listing must be clear, complete, correct, and rule-compliant【4†L203-L209】【28†L69-L77】. Some judgment (e.g. “Is the tone friendly enough?”) is allowed, but objective criteria (word counts, allowed vocabulary) anchor QC.

## 5. Deliverable-Specific Quality Standards  

- **Title Quality:** Titles must be concise and descriptive. Industry practice is ≤150 characters【6†L137-L142】【31†L269-L278】, in title case (capitalize major words) without ALL CAPS. Include essential info in order: *Brand Model – Key Features – Condition*.  For example, instead of “Blue Widget $15 BUY NOW BEST DEAL,” use *“BrandX Widget – 16GB – Like New”*.  Avoid repeated keywords or promotional language【6†L137-L142】【31†L275-L278】. Check for banned terms (e.g. “free,” “guaranteed”) per platform rules【31†L275-L278】.  A clear, accurate title directly impacts search visibility.  

- **Description Quality:** Descriptions should read like a helpful, benefits-focused narrative【3†L257-L264】. Begin by naming the item and its condition, then bullet or list key features (size, color, brand, functionality). Aim for thoroughness: typically 150+ words for major platforms【6†L165-L173】. Include any unique selling points or usage context. Use short paragraphs or bullets to improve readability【31†L306-L314】. Crucially, answer buyer questions: what’s included, the item’s history/condition, and why it’s valuable. Avoid technical overload and don’t just string specs with no context【31†L280-L287】. Always proofread: correct grammar and spelling are mandatory【9†L73-L81】.  

- **Pricing Recommendation Quality:** Recommendations must be **evidence-based**. Use a market-based approach: cite completed sales of comparable items (eBay sold listings, Poshmark results, etc.)【22†L263-L272】. Adjust price for condition and completeness (e.g. box, accessories)【22†L267-L272】【30†L428-L436】. State a suggested price or range with justification. If a quick sale is prioritized (high turnover), suggest pricing at/below the lower end of comps; if maximizing profit, set at/above the higher end【22†L269-L272】. Avoid arithmetic errors: remember to subtract fees/shipping when thinking of margins【30†L428-L436】. A sound pricing rationale (perhaps in footnotes or notes) is a hallmark of quality.  

- **Platform Recommendation Quality:** Advise the best marketplace(s) based on item type and audience. Justify your choice briefly: for example, “Electronics like this sell rapidly on eBay due to high buyer volume and auction format, whereas luxury apparel might yield higher prices on a boutique site.” Base this on research of user demographics, fees, and competition. (No single source outlines this specifically, so rely on market knowledge and data like “this category thrives on X” or [17†L124-L132] style research from similar platforms). Ensure the recommendation aligns with goal (fast sale vs high price) and platform rules.  

- **Category Recommendation Quality:** Choose the most specific valid category and subcategories for the item on the recommended platform. Precise categorization is crucial: miscategorizing drastically lowers visibility【14†L54-L63】【9†L57-L64】. Confirm using the marketplace’s taxonomy – for example, a “running shoe” should go under Sports→Shoes→Running, not a broad “Clothing” category. If uncertain, research seller forums or experiment in draft listings to find the right slot. Mention both the category name and (if relevant) category path to ensure no ambiguity.  

- **Photo-Order Guidance:** Guide the customer to capture and sequence images that maximize clarity. The first (cover) photo should be a full, in-focus shot of the item filling most of the frame【7†L125-L134】【9†L91-L97】. Subsequent shots should show all angles: front, back, sides, interior (if applicable), and any extra items (cords, manuals). Include detail shots of labels or flaws. Suggest at least 5–7 photos total (studies show conversion peaks around ~7 images【3†L354-L360】). Use white or neutral backgrounds for consistency【7†L136-L144】. Verify photos meet size requirements (ideally ≥1600px long edge【7†L125-L134】). The order might be: 1) main view, 2) alternate angle, 3) detail, 4) label/size tag, 5) in-context (e.g., next to a common object for scale). This structure helps buyers quickly assess the product.  

- **Posting Instructions:** Include any platform-specific “last mile” instructions so listings go live correctly. Ensure all mandatory fields are filled: condition, shipping options, return policy, item specifics, etc. Remind about tagging or bullet formatting rules if relevant. For example, if posting on eBay, verify item condition dropdown is correct and that shipping method is properly selected. If the platform uses tags or SEO fields, include relevant keywords (without duplication). Double-check alignment of title/desc with backend attributes (e.g. ensure “color=blue” matches the word “Blue” in text). Essentially, treat posting instructions as a final audit: no field should remain blank or inconsistent. (Note: incomplete attributes or wrong formats can block listings【28†L130-L138】, so mention that.)  

These deliverable-specific criteria should be codified: e.g. a title failing to mention brand or exceeding length is a quality violation; a description with promotional hype or missing critical specs fails; a pricing suggestion without rationale fails, etc. Each standard is measurable or verifiable against examples.

## 6. Most Common Errors and Failure Points  
Based on industry sources and seller experience, watch for these pitfalls:

- **Incomplete or Incorrect Data:** The most frequent mistake is missing details in titles/descriptions or leaving fields blank【28†L69-L77】. For example, omitting the brand name or key specs will confuse buyers and trigger low marketplace rankings【28†L69-L77】. Also watch for factual slip-ups (wrong model number, flipped size).  
- **Title/Description Violations:** Common title errors include exceeding length limits or using banned terms【31†L269-L278】. (Walmart forbids promo claims like “best deal”【6†L144-L152】.) Oversized or jumbled titles get suppressed. On the description side, over-stuffing with keywords or sharing irrelevant copy (like unrelated history) harms readability【31†L280-L287】.  
- **Stylistic Inconsistency:** Without a style guide, listings can have random tone shifts, inconsistent formatting, or capitalization errors. One seller noted that when they first outsourced work **without clear standards**, titles became inconsistent and descriptions rushed【3†L332-L340】. Typos and sloppy phrasing also slip through without spell-check.  
- **Photo Mistakes:** Failing to include enough photos or using low-res/poorly lit images. Forgetting a main shot (or using the wrong photo first) is a frequent oversight. As Pixelz emphasizes, marketplaces often *require* high-quality images【7†L69-L77】, so not meeting these guidelines (e.g. tiny item images on white) leads to poor engagement.  
- **Mispricing:** Pricing errors occur if comps aren’t checked or fees forgotten. For instance, forgetting to adjust for marketplace commissions will yield loss or underpricing. Another pitfall: Not aligning the pricing strategy with the item type (selling a collectible at a cookie-cutter price, then being surprised it didn’t sell).  
- **Category/Platform Mismatch:** Choosing a suboptimal platform or category is a weak point. If a listing is in the wrong category (as BrandMonkey warns, it “may not appear when customers search for it”【14†L54-L63】), sales vanish. Similarly, recommending a low-traffic marketplace by mistake hurts results.  
- **Compliance Oversights:** Errors like using trademarked terms incorrectly, including competitor brands, or ignoring ad guidelines. These can get a listing shut down【6†L144-L152】. Also watch for prohibited content (no obscene or irrelevant descriptions).  
- **Data Entry Failures:** A simple typo in price or dimensions, or switching condition from “New” to “Used” by mistake. These little data entry faults are surprisingly common.  

By focusing on these areas in QC, UtahLister can catch the “usual suspects.”  For instance, Productsup notes that *incomplete product listings* (wrong title/description) are among the most frequent listing errors【28†L69-L77】.  Keeping these failure modes in mind will help tailor the checklist and controls below.

## 7. Recommended Pre-Delivery QC Checklist  
We suggest a concise, step-by-step checklist (to be ticked off for every order) that covers all deliverables:

- **Title Check:** Confirm title length ≤150 chars【6†L137-L142】; contains all key attributes (brand, model, variant); no banned terms, all-caps, or promotional language【6†L137-L142】【31†L275-L278】. Capitalize properly.  
- **Description Check:** Ensure ≥150 words (if applicable) focusing on item’s features, condition and uses【6†L165-L173】. Verify brand, size, color, model year, etc., are mentioned. Check grammar/spelling (use a spellchecker). Eliminate any mention of competitors or unsourced claims. If using bullets, verify formatting for the platform.  
- **Pricing Check:** Confirm price aligns with market research (compare with at least 2–3 sold listings)【22†L263-L272】. Ensure any fee/shipping considerations are included in profit margin. If a range, ensure high/low bounds are realistic.  
- **Platform & Category Check:** Verify the chosen marketplace suits this item (based on category, target customer). Confirm the category and subcategory exactly match the item【14†L54-L63】【9†L57-L64】. All mandatory item-specific fields (attributes) are filled (e.g. size, condition, material) as recommended by [4] and [28].  
- **Photo Check:** Ensure there are enough images (recommend ≥5) of good quality【3†L354-L360】【7†L58-L63】. The first image clearly shows the product; additional images cover different angles, details, and any flaws. All images have clean backgrounds and meet resolution guidelines (preferably ≥1600px on longest side【7†L125-L134】).  
- **Post Details Check:** Before posting, fill all required fields (price set, shipping options selected, return policy updated). Confirm that description matches the content tags (e.g. if “blue” is in title, the color field is “Blue”). Make sure no template placeholders remain and that the listing preview looks correct.  
- **Compliance & Style Check:** Review the draft for any prohibited language (no “% off”, “free shipping” in title【31†L275-L278】, etc.). Ensure punctuation, caps, and measurement units follow platform rules (e.g. spell out “ounce” instead of “oz” if required【31†L306-L314】).  
- **Overall Consistency Check:** Finally, read the listing as a potential buyer. It should make sense end-to-end. The tone should match UtahLister’s brand voice. Ensure links or attachments aren’t accidentally included.  

Each item in this checklist should be checked off (ideally in writing or via a form) before approving a listing for delivery.  The references above indicate that missing any of these (e.g. leaving a required attribute blank【28†L69-L77】 or exceeding title length【6†L137-L142】) would degrade quality.

## 8. Recommended Pass / Fail Standards  
UtahLister needs clear pass/fail criteria so drafts aren’t prematurely delivered. We recommend treating **any violation of an objective requirement as “Fail”**. In practice this means:

- **Fail (requires fix)** if *any* of these occur:  
  - Title or description includes disallowed content (promotions, competitor mentions, explicit material)【6†L144-L152】【31†L275-L278】.  
  - Title >150 characters or missing critical info (like brand/model)【6†L137-L142】.  
  - Description missing key item facts (e.g. omits item condition, dimensions, or brand).  
  - Fewer than the minimum acceptable images (e.g. <5 or missing a main view)【3†L354-L360】.  
  - Price recommendation appears illogical (far outside market range) or no justification.  
  - Category or platform choice is obviously wrong (e.g. listing a laptop under “Furniture”).  
  - Any factual error (wrong model #, flipped numbers) or data typo.  
  - Format noncompliance (e.g. use of bullets when the platform forbids it) or failure to fill mandatory field.  

- **Pass (ready for delivery)** if all major checklist items are satisfied and no fail conditions above apply. Small subjective tweaks (e.g. “this sentence could be more persuasive”) would not constitute a fail – but missing mandatory content or rule breaches do.  

We emphasize **“zero tolerance”** for objective failures. As one Walmart policy note warns, noncompliance can lead to listing removal【6†L118-L126】.  Thus, any “Fail” item must be corrected before delivery.  This pass/fail logic ensures UtahLister’s output never slips below the agreed-upon bar.

## 9. Recommended Internal Service Controls  
To prevent recurring mistakes, UtahLister should implement these controls:

- **Checklist Enforcement:** Integrate the above QC checklist into the workflow (perhaps as a digital form or printed list). No listing goes out without ticking all boxes. This reduces human memory errors.  
- **Style Guide & Templates:** Maintain a living style guide (tone, grammar rules, trademark policies) and use template snippets for standard language (e.g. a formatted dimensions line). This improves consistency. [3] suggests using saved templates for common fields and even automated attribute-filling tools【3†L268-L274】. For example, pre-fill common attributes (brand, size chart, shipping note) and customize per item.  
- **Automated Checks:** Use available tools to catch errors. Spell-check and grammar apps can reduce typos. One seller uses AI to flag missing details or low-res images【3†L319-L327】 – we should explore similar, such as image-resolution scanners or scripts that verify required fields are nonempty. Even a simple spreadsheet or Google Form can enforce “no blanks allowed.”  
- **Random Audits:** Regularly sample past listings for a quality review (e.g. 10 random listings per week【3†L319-L327】). This audit should use the same checklist and identify any patterns of failure. These audits drive learning: if a certain type of error recurs, the process or training should be updated. SGP Group advises regular process review to catch new issues【41†L38-L44】.  
- **Error Log and Feedback Loop:** Track defects found (see defect types below) in a shared log. After each delivery, note any client feedback or required fixes. Over time this data reveals weak spots. For instance, if multiple revisions cite “description unclear”, UtahLister might refine the description guidelines.  
- **Version Control:** Keep dated copies of SOPs or templates. If a revision occurs, note why and update the guideline so it’s not forgotten.  
- **Time Buffers:** Since it’s a solo operation, build in review time. For example, finish a draft a few hours before delivery deadline to allow a fresh QC pass (not rushing at the last minute). This simple control avoids sloppy last-minute errors.  

These internal controls combine **process** (checklist, audits) and **tools** (templates, automation). They aim to catch preventable errors before client sees them. [3]’s example of using technology (automated attribute fill, AI flagging) highlights that even a solo operator can leverage small automations to avoid easy mistakes【3†L268-L274】【3†L319-L327】.  

## 10. Revision, Rework, and Defect Logic  
We distinguish two types of post-delivery changes: **Defects** (failures to meet the agreed standards) and **Preference changes** (extra requests). Our logic:

- **Defect (no-charge fix):** Any item that fails the pass/fail criteria above (section 8) is a defect. For example, a missing dimension line, a factual mistake, or a forbidden word appearing is a defect. These are errors in the deliverable relative to the QC specifications. Defects must be corrected immediately at UtahLister’s expense – the client expected the agreed standard【35†L657-L660】.  
- **Revision (potential extra work):** A client’s request for a different emphasis, new photo placement, or stylistic change that was not promised is a preference change. This is not a defect by QC standards, but rather an alteration of scope. Such changes should be handled as formal revisions. They may require extra time or fees, akin to a “change request” in consulting parlance【35†L657-L660】. 

This follows the approach of specification-driven projects【35†L657-L660】: clearly categorize an issue based on the specification (here, our QC standards). Defects are fixed immediately (covered by UtahLister’s scope); true change requests can be billed or negotiated. For example, if a client simply wants a snappier title after seeing the draft, that’s a preference change. But if the title used a prohibited term or omitted a required detail, that’s a defect requiring correction.  

Internally, any revision request should be logged separately from defects. Over time, this helps UtahLister see if issues are genuine errors or evolving client taste. We recommend documenting “defect rules” (e.g., “Brand name must always be included in title” – omitting it is a defect) and “revision policy” (e.g., “Tone tweaks count as revisions”). This clarity prevents disputes and ensures consistent handling of post-delivery work.

## 11. Recommended Quality Metrics to Track  
To gauge performance and continuous improvement, track these metrics over time:

- **First-Pass Yield (Pass Rate):** Percentage of listings that pass the QC checklist on the first attempt. (Or conversely, *defect rate*: % needing fixes.) A rising yield indicates better consistency.  
- **Defect and Revision Counts:** Number of defects found per listing and number of client revision requests per listing. Track by category (e.g. “Title defects”, “Price complaints”) to spot trouble areas. Software testing uses similar KPIs (see acceptance test stats with passes/fails【35†L673-L681】).  
- **Turnaround Time:** Average time from order to final delivery (including QC). If time is slipping, it may signal process inefficiency or insufficient resources.  
- **Client Satisfaction:** Simple proxies like repeat orders or direct feedback can gauge if quality meets expectations. A net promoter-like score (e.g. on a scale of 1–10 how satisfied the client is) can be tracked if feasible.  
- **Error Trend Analysis:** Are the number of QC errors trending down? Ideally we see fewer defects of a given type as we refine processes. Monitoring this helps confirm that controls (checklist, templates) are working.  
- **Delivery Timeliness:** On-time vs late delivery rate. Even perfect quality fails if delivered late.  

While specific numeric targets can be set later, the focus now is on establishing the habit of measurement. One can think of this as akin to how acceptance tests report “passed vs failed” counts【35†L673-L681】; here we track “accepted vs corrected” listings. Over time, such metrics become actionable: if defect rates spike, inspect why.

## 12. Biggest Quality-Control Risks  
The main risks to watch out for include:

- **Unclear Standards:** Without crystal-clear definitions of quality, consistency evaporates. As one QC guide warns, failing to “clearly define what ‘good quality’ means” is a top mistake【41†L29-L36】. UtahLister must resist any ambiguity; every rule (e.g. title length, required photo count) should be spelled out.  
- **Complacency / No Audits:** Skipping regular reviews is risky. SGP Group notes that not reviewing processes regularly allows inefficiencies and defects to persist【41†L38-L44】. If UtahLister assumes “it’s obvious” and never audits past work, errors will slip through repeatedly.  
- **Relying Only on Final Checks:** Doing QC only at the end of listing creation (or trusting only self-inspection without tools) is a mistake【41†L46-L54】. Problems should be caught during each stage (writing, researching, formatting) not just after everything’s done. For example, checking the title only after the whole description is written invites oversights.  
- **Ignoring Feedback:** Overlooking client or buyer feedback means missing a key perspective. A QC mistake list explicitly calls out “ignoring customer feedback” as harmful【41†L55-L62】. UtahLister should treat client revision notes as valuable data, not as nuisances.  
- **Over-Engineering QC:** In a solo shop, a too-complex QC system (e.g. dozens of pages of rules) might not be sustainable. The risk is building controls that are too heavy to use. Avoid this by keeping checklists short and automating where possible.  
- **Scope Creep in QC:** Defining too many subjective “nice-to-have” items as mandatory creates conflict. For instance, if every client has a different style, rigidity can cause friction. We mitigate this by clearly labeling subjective expectations as guidelines rather than pass/fail rules.  

In summary, the biggest risks are process gaps (undefined standards, no reviews) and not listening to data. To **not** fall into these traps, UtahLister must maintain disciplined documentation, regular audits, and a mindset of continuous learning【41†L29-L36】【41†L38-L44】.

## 13. Recommended Official QC Framework for UtahLister Right Now  
For the present, we suggest a **lightweight, spec-driven QC framework** with these components:

1. **Defined Standards Document:** Combine the above criteria into an official guide (this report’s content). Think of it as a “Listing QA Spec” that every deliverable is measured against. Include objective rules (e.g. title length) and clear examples (as in [3]’s Nike title example) to remove ambiguity.  

2. **Checklist Enforcement:** Use the checklist from Section 7 as the heart of the framework. Each order must pass this checklist. Treat it as a gating step: nothing moves on to “client-ready” until the checklist is all green.  

3. **Iterative Review Cycle:** Before handing off a draft, do a self-audit. Then, after delivery and any initial feedback, perform a short retrospective: update the defect log, note any necessary standard tweaks, and revise the checklist if needed.  

4. **Metrics and Tracking:** Begin logging the key metrics (defect counts, pass rates). Even a simple spreadsheet can track them. Review these metrics weekly. This creates accountability and highlights quality trends.  

5. **Role of Founder (Quality Owner):** The founder (or designated quality lead) will own this framework: updating standards, checking audits, and deciding when to tighten or relax criteria. This ensures that QC scales with growing volume.  

6. **Tools & Automation:** To the extent possible, automate repetitive checks (grammar, required fields). This framework should identify opportunities to save time without skipping steps.  

7. **Continuous Feedback Loop:** At any point, if a client flags an issue as a defect, it’s fed back into the system (adding to the defect types in our DB). If clients often request a certain style change, consider whether it should be a new standard.  

This framework aims to be robust yet feasible for one person. It resembles an agile “test-and-improve” cycle: define quality specs, test (QC check), fix if needed, learn, then repeat. Notably, it borrows from software QA practices【35†L657-L660】 (specifications driving QA) adapted for listing services.

  - Compliance (no banned language or format violations)  
  - Consistency (stylistic uniformity)  

- **QC Checklist Items:**  
  1. Title ≤150 chars, includes brand/model, no banned terms【6†L137-L142】【31†L275-L278】.  
  2. Description ≥150 words (if required), covers all item attributes (brand, condition, color, etc.)【6†L165-L173】.  
  3. Pricing justified by 2+ comparable sales【22†L263-L272】.  
  4. Images: ≥5 photos, main view + key angles, white/clean background【7†L125-L134】【9†L91-L97】.  
  5. Category matches product (double-checked against marketplace taxonomy)【14†L54-L63】.  
  6. No spelling/grammar errors (use a tool).  
  7. No prohibited words/claims (e.g. no “free”, no retailer names)【6†L144-L152】.  
  8. All required fields filled (return policy, shipping, item specifics)【28†L130-L138】.  

- **Pass/Fail Criteria:**  
  - *Pass:* All checklist items satisfied.  
  - *Fail:* Any checklist failure (e.g. missing brand, banned term, insufficient photos).  
  - *Defect:* A fail due to missing promised content or error.  
  - *Revision:* Any client-requested change outside the agreed spec.  

- **Defect Types:**  
  - Missing or incorrect item specification (e.g. “Size field blank”).  
  - Title violations (length, banned term).  
  - Description omissions (no condition described).  
  - Inaccurate price (out-of-range without explanation).  
  - Image quality failures (blurry, wrong background).  
  - Category/platform error (wrong assignment).  
  - Format mistakes (bullet where prohibited, HTML tags visible).  

- **Revision vs Defect Distinctions:**  
  - *Defect:* Fails quality standard (e.g. forgot to mention included accessories). Fix by correcting that info.  
  - *Revision:* New request (e.g. “Can you change the tone to be more upbeat?”). May require new approval.  
  - Defects are fixed free; revisions are managed by separate request.  

- **Control Mechanisms:**  
  - Mandatory QC checklist sign-off before delivery.  
  - Scheduled audits (e.g. weekly review of past listings).  
  - Use of templates for common fields.  
  - Automation (spell-check, image-resolution tools).  
  - Defect log to record and analyze errors.  

- **Quality Metrics to Track:**  
  - First-pass success rate (%) vs lists needing rework.  
  - Number of defects per 100 listings.  
  - Number of client revisions per 100 listings.  
  - Average turnaround time (order to delivery).  
  - Customer satisfaction score (if captured).
