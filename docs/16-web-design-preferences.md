# UtahLister Web Design Preferences

## Core preference summary

The site should feel like a clean, trustworthy, premium landing page for a
real service business, not a portfolio experiment, upload tool, or abstract
creative layout.

The first screen must make it immediately obvious:
- what the company is
- what the service does
- why the visitor should trust the operator
- why UtahLister will likely produce a stronger listing than the visitor would

## What the user explicitly dislikes

- layouts that feel like a generic photo-upload or utility tool
- unclear first impression
- weak service framing
- strange or gimmicky composition choices
- visual ideas that make the brand or purpose harder to understand
- design that does not look clean, current, trustworthy, or polished
- a site that feels too abstract or too soft to sell a real service

## What the user wants

- immediate clarity
- strong trust and professionalism
- modern and trend-aware design
- visual polish that feels advanced and capable
- a style that appeals to both men and women
- a slight feminine edge without becoming niche or overly girly
- obvious proof that the operator can outperform the average seller
- a landing page that feels sellable to strangers
- premium but practical energy
- brand-first presentation instead of a founder-personality-first presentation
- fast comprehension of the service and value proposition
- clear evidence that UtahLister can handle the work end to end
- a CTA that feels accessible and action-oriented, not intimidating

## Messaging direction

- the opening sentence should communicate that UtahLister can help items sell
  faster and more effectively than the average seller would manage alone
- the tone should be persuasive, polished, and inviting
- the tone should not feel pushy, cheesy, scammy, or overtly "salesy"
- copy should use high-conviction, high-clarity persuasion rather than gimmicky
  or obviously manipulative language
- the service should feel worth paying for because it saves time, reduces effort,
  and improves the quality of the final listing

## Trust and conversion priorities

The first screen should quickly signal:
- done-for-you service
- speed and convenience
- stronger listing quality than a casual seller usually creates
- practical resale expertise
- easy next step

Most important trust themes right now:
- ability to fully handle the work
- speed and convenience
- strong outcomes and proof

## Reference reactions

### Sites the user reacted against

- `rosscommerce.com`
  - positive: the service gist is clear quickly
  - negative: too formal and expensive-feeling
  - negative: disliked the main image

- `amrutsoftware.com`
  - negative: too overloaded and congested
  - negative: hard to understand quickly

- `automatedinvestmentgroup.com`
  - negative: felt like a cheesy infomercial
  - negative: low trust

### Sites/features the user reacted positively to

- `channelbloom.com`
  - positive: booking/scheduling option made the business feel real and accessible
  - negative: still slightly too congested

User described the ideal direction as something like:
- if example 1 and example 4 had a baby
- ultra straightforward
- clean and modern
- tells the viewer what it can do quickly
- shows proof quickly

### Visual references the user gravitated toward

- clean hero composition
- premium, controlled modernity
- strong framing and confident typography
- a little mystery is acceptable if the page still feels legible and high-trust
- visuals should feel elevated, but the purpose of the site must still be obvious

## Design implications

- brand and service promise must be obvious above the fold
- hero should read like a high-end done-for-you service business
- imagery should support proof and competence, not obscure the message
- proof and before/after outcomes should appear early
- copy should emphasize expertise, quality, and conversion outcomes
- composition should be clean and legible before it tries to be clever
- the page should feel more like a premium local service brand than a creative demo
- the page should not feel like a SaaS product, uploader, dashboard, or agency for enterprise clients
- the page should feel approachable enough that a normal local seller would actually reach out
- proof should be visible early, but presented cleanly rather than as a busy wall of stats

## Open design direction to validate

- how luxurious vs practical the brand should feel
- whether the visual reference should lean editorial, premium service, or modern ecommerce
- how bold the feminine edge should be
- how much of the page should focus on proof vs direct service explanation
- whether the CTA should stay form-first, add a scheduling option, or offer both
